COPD Medications

Your Guide to COPD Medications

Understanding your medications is a key part of managing your COPD and breathing your best.

Bronchodilators: Opening Your Airways

These are the cornerstone of COPD treatment. They work by relaxing the muscles around your airways, helping them to open up and making it easier to breathe. They come in short-acting and long-acting forms.

‚ö°Ô∏è Short-Acting Beta-Agonists (SABAs)

+

How it Works: Quickly relaxes airway muscles. Often called "rescue inhalers."

When it's Used: As needed for sudden shortness of breath. Effects last 4-6 hours.

Examples: Albuterol (ProAir, Ventolin), Levalbuterol (Xopenex).

Common Side Effects: Tremors, fast heartbeat, nervousness.

Special Notes: If you need to use your rescue inhaler more than 2-3 times a week, your COPD may not be well-controlled. Tell your doctor.

üí® Short-Acting Muscarinic Antagonists (SAMAs)

+

How it Works: Prevents airway muscles from tightening. Also a type of "rescue inhaler."

When it's Used: As needed for sudden breathlessness, sometimes in combination with a SABA.

Examples: Ipratropium (Atrovent).

Common Side Effects: Dry mouth, cough.

Special Notes: Can be a good alternative or add-on for people who are sensitive to the side effects of SABAs.

‚è≥ Long-Acting Beta-Agonists (LABAs)

+

How it Works: Relaxes airway muscles for an extended period.

When it's Used: On a regular daily schedule to control symptoms. Effects last 12-24 hours. These are "maintenance" inhalers.

Examples: Salmeterol (Serevent), Formoterol (Perforomist), Indacaterol (Arcapta).

Common Side Effects: Similar to SABAs, but less common. Can include headache and throat irritation.

Special Notes: Not for sudden symptoms. Must be taken every day as prescribed, even if you feel well.

üõ°Ô∏è Long-Acting Muscarinic Antagonists (LAMAs)

+

How it Works: Prevents airway muscles from tightening. Also helps reduce mucus production.

When it's Used: On a regular daily schedule for maintenance. Effects last 12-24 hours.

Examples: Tiotropium (Spiriva), Umeclidinium (Incruse Ellipta), Aclidinium (Tudorza).

Common Side Effects: Dry mouth is the most common. Some may experience urinary retention.

Special Notes: A very effective class of maintenance medication for COPD. Let your doctor know if you have glaucoma or an enlarged prostate.

Anti-Inflammatories: Reducing Swelling

These medications reduce the inflammation and swelling in your airways, which can help decrease mucus production and prevent flare-ups.

üî• Inhaled Corticosteroids (ICS)

+

How it Works: A steroid that directly targets the airways to reduce inflammation.

When it's Used: As a daily maintenance medication, almost always in combination with a LABA. Used for patients with frequent flare-ups.

Examples: Fluticasone (Flovent), Budesonide (Pulmicort), Mometasone (Asmanex).

Common Side Effects: Hoarse voice, sore throat, and oral thrush (a fungal infection in the mouth).

Special Notes: It is very important to rinse your mouth with water and spit it out after each use to prevent thrush. ICS may slightly increase the risk of pneumonia in some patients.

üíä PDE4 Inhibitors

+

How it Works: An oral pill that reduces airway inflammation from the inside out.

When it's Used: Daily for severe COPD with chronic bronchitis and a history of frequent flare-ups.

Examples: Roflumilast (Daliresp).

Common Side Effects: Diarrhea, nausea, weight loss, and headache, especially when starting.

Special Notes: Not a bronchodilator. It can take several weeks to start working effectively.

Combination Inhalers: The Power of Teamwork

Many people with COPD benefit from using more than one type of medication. Combination inhalers deliver two or three different drugs in a single device, making treatment simpler and more effective.

ü§ù LABA + LAMA

+

How it Works: Combines two different long-acting bronchodilators to open airways in two different ways.

Examples: Umeclidinium/vilanterol (Anoro Ellipta), Tiotropium/olodaterol (Stiolto Respimat).

Special Notes: A very common and effective maintenance therapy for many COPD patients.

‚ûï LABA + ICS

+

How it Works: Combines a long-acting bronchodilator with an anti-inflammatory steroid.

Examples: Fluticasone/salmeterol (Advair), Budesonide/formoterol (Symbicort).

Special Notes: Generally for patients who have frequent flare-ups. Remember to rinse your mouth after use.

üéØ Triple Therapy (LAMA + LABA + ICS)

+

How it Works: Delivers two types of bronchodilators plus a steroid in one inhaler.

Examples: Fluticasone/umeclidinium/vilanterol (Trelegy Ellipta).

Special Notes: For patients with more severe COPD and continued flare-ups despite other treatments.

Other Important Medications

üíß Mucolytics

+

How it Works: Thins mucus in the airways, making it less thick and sticky, and easier to cough up.

When it's Used: As a daily oral medication for people with chronic bronchitis and thick mucus.

Examples: Carbocisteine, N-acetylcysteine (NAC).

Special Notes: Helps reduce the frequency of flare-ups in some patients.

üö® Flare-Up Treatments

+

Antibiotics: Not for daily use. Prescribed by your doctor to treat bacterial infections during a flare-up.

Oral Corticosteroids: Powerful steroid pills (like Prednisone) used for short periods to control severe flare-ups.

Your Best Defense: Vaccinations

While not a direct treatment for COPD, staying up-to-date on vaccinations is one of the most important things you can do to stay healthy. Lung infections can cause severe flare-ups, so prevention is key.

Technique is Everything!

The best medication in the world won't work if it doesn't get to your lungs. There are many different types of inhaler devices. Ask your doctor, pharmacist, or respiratory therapist to show you how to use your specific inhaler correctly and to check your technique at every visit.
