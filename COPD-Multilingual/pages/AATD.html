AATD & COPD

The Genetic Risk: Alpha-1 and COPD

Discover how a hidden genetic condition can lead to lung disease, and why smoking makes it far more dangerous.

What is Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency?

Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency (AATD or Alpha-1) is an inherited genetic condition. It means the body doesn't produce enough of a protein called alpha-1 antitrypsin (AAT). This protein is made in the liver and travels to the lungs to perform a vital job.

Its main function is to protect the lungs from damage caused by inflammation, especially from an enzyme called neutrophil elastase, which our own immune cells release to fight infection.

Understanding the Genes

The most common normal gene is 'M'. The most common faulty genes that cause AATD are 'S' and 'Z'. A person with two 'M' genes (MM) is normal. A person with two 'Z' genes (ZZ) has severe AATD. Other combinations (like MS or SZ) result in varying levels of risk.

How is it Inherited?

Everyone inherits two AAT genes, one from each parent. If you inherit one normal gene and one faulty gene, you are a "carrier" but usually make enough protein to stay healthy. If you inherit two faulty genes, you have AATD.

Beyond the Lungs: Alpha-1 and Liver Disease

ü©∫

In the most common form of AATD, the faulty AAT protein gets 'stuck' in the liver cells where it's made. This buildup can damage the liver over time, leading to scarring (fibrosis or cirrhosis) and increasing the risk for liver cancer. For some people with Alpha-1, the liver problems can be more serious than the lung problems.

A Devastating Combination: AATD and Smoking

If AATD leaves the lungs with a weakened shield, smoking is like inviting an entire army to attack. Tobacco smoke not only causes massive inflammation (more attackers), but it also deactivates any AAT protein that is present (damaging the shield itself). This one-two punch leads to extremely rapid and severe lung destruction.

Specific Treatment: Augmentation Therapy

For individuals with AATD-related lung damage, a specific treatment called augmentation therapy is available. This involves weekly intravenous (IV) infusions of the AAT protein, collected from the plasma of healthy human donors. The goal is not to cure COPD or reverse existing damage, but to increase the level of AAT protein in the lungs to protect them from further destruction.

This therapy is typically considered for individuals with AATD who have evidence of emphysema. Your doctor can determine if you are an eligible candidate.

Should You Be Tested?

Because Alpha-1 is under-recognized, the World Health Organization recommends that all people diagnosed with COPD, regardless of age or smoking history, should be tested. A simple blood test can diagnose the condition. Testing is especially critical if you:

  • Have COPD, emphysema, or bronchiectasis.
  • Developed COPD at a young age (under 45).
  • Have a family history of AATD or early-onset lung disease.
  • Have unexplained liver disease.
