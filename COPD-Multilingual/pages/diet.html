COPD Nutrition

Fuel Your Body, Fuel Your Breath

Learn how the right foods can give you more energy and make breathing easier.

Why is Nutrition So Important for COPD?

For people with COPD, the simple act of breathing can burn up to 10 times more calories than for someone without lung disease. This means your body needs the right fuel to power your breathing muscles and maintain a healthy weight.

Furthermore, the food you eat affects the amount of carbon dioxide (CO2) your body produces. Your lungs have to work harder to remove CO2, so choosing foods that produce less CO2 can make a real difference in your breathing.

Managing Your Weight

If You Are Underweight...

Being underweight can weaken your muscles (including breathing muscles) and your immune system. Focus on nutrient-dense foods.

  • Add healthy fats like olive oil and avocado to meals.
  • Snack on nuts, seeds, and cheese.
  • Consider adding milk powder to dishes like soups and casseroles for extra calories and protein.

If You Are Overweight...

Excess weight can make it harder for your lungs and heart to work. The goal is to lose weight slowly and steadily with a balanced diet.

  • Focus on non-starchy vegetables, lean proteins, and whole grains.
  • Limit added sugars, simple carbs, and fried foods.
  • Work with your doctor or a dietitian to create a safe plan that combines diet with gentle exercise.

Eat Smaller, More Frequent Meals

Try 4-6 small meals instead of 3 large ones. A full stomach can press on your diaphragm, making it harder to breathe.

If you get tired while eating, try resting for a few minutes before you start your meal.

Sit Upright

Sit up straight while you eat to give your lungs more room to expand.

The Importance of Hydration

üíß

Staying well-hydrated is crucial. Drinking enough fluids (especially water) helps keep the mucus in your airways thin and easier to clear. This can reduce coughing and the risk of infections.

  • Aim for 6-8 glasses of non-caffeinated fluids per day, unless your doctor advises otherwise.
  • Drink between meals, not during, to avoid feeling too full to eat.
  • Limit caffeine and alcohol as they can dehydrate you and interfere with medications.

Talk to Your Healthcare Team

Your nutritional needs are unique. Always talk to your doctor or a registered dietitian to create a meal plan that is perfect for you. They can help you manage your weight and ensure you're getting the right nutrients to feel your best.
